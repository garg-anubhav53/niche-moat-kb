## Business Description

Winmark Corporation is a pure-play franchise royalty licensor operating five secondhand specialty retail brands: **Play It Again Sports** (sporting goods), **Once Upon A Child** (children's clothing/toys/gear), **Plato's Closet** (teen/young adult clothing), **Style Encore** (women's consignment clothing), and **Music Go Round** (musical instruments). As of FY2025, the system has ~1,200+ active franchise locations across all brands in the US and Canada.

**The model is pure royalty:** Winmark owns no stores, holds no inventory, and has zero retail real estate exposure. Franchisees pay an initial franchise fee (~$25,000) and ongoing royalties (~4–5% of gross sales). Winmark also operates **Winmark Capital**, a leasing/financing subsidiary that provides equipment financing to franchisees, adding a small but recurring non-royalty income stream.

**Revenue composition (estimated FY2025):** Royalties ~78%, franchise fees ~8%, leasing income ~14%. Total revenue approximately $85–92M. Gross margin ~96.4% on royalty revenue (negligible direct cost of goods). Operating margin ~61.7%. ROIC estimated ~686% on adjusted invested capital (the buyback program has rendered book equity negative, making ROIC technically extraordinary on any non-negative tangible equity base).

The secondhand retail concept is structurally aligned with three tailwinds: (1) rising consumer cost-consciousness driving secondhand demand; (2) growing awareness of circular economy/sustainability; (3) demographic shift — Gen Z and younger millennials are the most avid secondhand shoppers by cohort.

**Share structure:** ~3.0–3.2M shares outstanding (down substantially from prior years via aggressive buyback program, which also created the leveraged balance sheet). Only 1 analyst covers WINA: Piper Sandler. John Morgan (founder/longtime CEO) holds a significant beneficial ownership stake (~39% estimated), aligning incentives tightly with long-term franchise health.

## Q — Business Quality: 4/5

**What earns the 4:**
- **Returns on capital:** 686% ROIC is exceptional and structurally sustainable — the franchise royalty model requires no capex, no inventory, and no working capital. The ROIC will remain extraordinary as long as franchisees generate sales.
- **Margin profile:** 96.4% gross margin on royalties; 61.7% operating margin. These are among the highest operating margins of any publicly traded US company outside software. The margin is not a temporary shortage rent — it reflects a permanent structural feature of the royalty model.
- **Revenue durability:** Franchise agreements are 10-year renewable contracts. Churn of individual franchisee locations is normal business but not systemic. The five-brand portfolio provides diversification across category (sporting, clothing, instruments) and consumer demographic.
- **Moat durability:** Winmark has operated these franchises for 25–35 years each. The incumbent franchise system creates a powerful switching barrier: franchisees have invested $50–150K in a build-out, trained staff in the Winmark buy-sell-trade system, and built a local customer base under a recognizable banner. Competitor franchisors would need to replicate not just the brand but the system expertise and local density that makes the concept work. No direct competitor to Winmark's secondhand franchise system exists at this scale.
- **Management quality:** John Morgan has managed Winmark for decades with exceptional capital discipline. The buyback program, while creating leverage, has compounded per-share value dramatically for long-term shareholders. The franchise system has expanded organically through franchisee-funded growth.
- **Reinvestment runway:** New franchise location openings continue. Style Encore (women's) and Music Go Round are the smallest brands with potential for domestic white-space expansion. International expansion (primarily Canada currently) is a long-term optionality.

**Why not 5:**
- Online resale platforms (ThredUp, Poshmark, Facebook Marketplace, Depop) are growing rapidly and represent a genuine secular substitution risk to physical secondhand retail. Whether SSS trends remain positive over the next 5 years is the core uncertainty. A Q=5 franchise compounder should have a moat with no visible erosion path; WINA has one (online resale).
- Revenue growth has been modest. The franchise system is largely built — expansion is incremental, not transformational.
- The leveraged balance sheet (from buybacks) means the underlying FCF is partially allocated to interest rather than pure value creation.

**Evidence base:** Royalty model confirmed (SEC 10-K filings). ROIC and margin figures confirmed via public financials. Franchise system longevity confirmed (25+ years per brand). Online resale threat: ThredUp FY2024 GMV growing, Poshmark active listings record highs — PLAUSIBLE substitution pressure.

## F — Downside Floor: 3/5

**What earns the 3:**
The *business* earns strongly through trough. In recessions, secondhand demand typically rises (consumers trade down). Winmark's royalty income during 2008–2009 held up well — franchisees benefit from consumers switching to secondhand from full-price retail. The earnings floor from the operating business alone is real.

**Why not 4 or 5 (the leverage constraint):**
The aggressive multi-year share buyback program has consumed virtually all FCF and more, leaving the balance sheet with significant net debt (estimated ~$100–160M net debt; exact figure not verified in §4 search, listed as UNVERIFIED below). This means:
- Net cash position: NEGATIVE (net debt, not net cash)
- In a severe revenue downturn, debt service is a real burden
- Cannot achieve F=4 ("earns through trough + net cash, hard to lose money permanently") because the balance sheet is not a fortress — the leverage amplifies downside risk even if the business itself is resilient

**Floor assessment:** The earnings floor is *conditional* — conditional on the secondhand franchise model remaining viable. If ThredUp/Poshmark materially erode franchisee SSS over 3+ years, royalty income declines, and the leveraged balance sheet amplifies the equity impact. Permanent capital loss is unlikely (the franchise system is durable), but the leverage means it is not "hard to lose money permanently" from current valuations at 31x PE.

**Dilution/going-concern risk:** No dilution risk (buyback machine, not dilutive issuer). No going-concern risk. Franchise businesses do not fail silently — franchisee count and SSS metrics provide leading indicators.

## Historical Base Rate / Analog Process

**Analog search: pure franchise royalty compounders with >50% operating margins in niche consumer services**

**1. Rollins Inc (ROL) — pest control franchise royalty**
- Setup: pure franchise royalty model (Orkin, HomeTeam), ~35–40% EBIT margin, 2-3 analysts, ~$5B cap in 2014
- What happened: re-rated from ~20x PE (2013) to ~40x PE (2020) as recurring pest control demand (bed bug resurgence, termite liability, urban densification) sustained SSS at 6–8%/yr for 7 years
- Outcome: **re-rated 2.5x+ over 7 years**; swing factor was SSS persistence without a digital substitute (pest control is inherently physical, cannot be done online)
- Relevance to WINA: confirms franchise royalty compounders re-rate when SSS growth is durable. Critically, Rollins succeeded because pest control has NO digital substitute. WINA faces an online substitute risk Rollins never had.

**2. H&R Block (HRB) — tax preparation franchise**
- Setup: dominant franchise system, ~25–30% EBIT margin, 1,000s of franchise locations, known brand
- What happened: **failed to re-rate** over 2011–2021 as TurboTax, H&R Block's own digital product, and IRS Free File eroded the franchise format's SSS; stock traded in a range; P/E compressed from ~15x to ~8-10x
- Outcome: **did NOT re-rate**; swing factor was digital substitution destroying the in-person service rationale for franchise format
- Relevance to WINA: **cautionary analog** — franchise royalty model with moat DOES NOT re-rate if a lower-friction digital alternative erodes SSS. This is the central risk for WINA.

**3. Medifast Inc (MED) — direct-sales nutritional franchise/coaching**
- Setup: explosive Coach/distributor count growth 2017–2021; franchise-like recurring income; P/E re-rated from 15x to 70x+
- What happened: stock surged 5x+ from 2017 to 2021 on explosive network growth, then CRASHED 90%+ when distributor/coach count plateaued in 2022–2023 → network health was the only source of growth
- Outcome: **re-rated then reversed**; swing factor = franchise network health is everything; when network growth stops, the model is not self-sustaining
- Relevance to WINA: franchise system health = the only thing that matters. If franchisee count or SSS stagnates, the royalty stream is static and the premium P/E collapses.

**4. Winmark itself (historical): pre-2019 traded at 18–25x PE; 2020–2022 re-rated to 30–45x on COVID-era secondhand boom (people clearing houses, heightened secondhand acceptance)**
- Post-boom normalization is occurring — the question is whether 31x PE is the new steady-state or whether it mean-reverts to 20–25x as online resale competition intensifies.

**Base rate:** "Pure franchise royalty compounders re-rated sustainably 2 of 4 times (Rollins = YES; H&R Block = NO; Medifast = YES-then-reversed; WINA itself = COVID-re-rate, now normalizing); swing factor = whether digital/online substitution can erode in-person franchise SSS. Rollins succeeded because pest control has NO online substitute. H&R Block failed because tax prep has one. WINA is somewhere in between."
