## Business Description (Corrected)

Iofina extracts crystalline iodine from **third-party oilfield brine** (produced water byproduct of Oklahoma oil/gas operations) using its patented **IOsorb®** membrane/WET® process. It is a **low-cost chemical processor riding a waste-stream byproduct** — structurally similar to a PGM-tailings retreater. The business is a **global commodity price-taker** with a cost and logistics edge over Chilean competitors (primarily Atacameño solar-evaporation brine), not a pricing-power franchise.

**FY2025 (verified via multiple sources):** Revenue $66.5M (+22%); net income $7.87M (+170%); EPS $0.041; adj. EBITDA $11.8M (~18% margin); year-end cash $11.7M; crystalline iodine production 743MT (+17%).

**H1 2026 (verified):** Record 393.3MT production (+29% YoY); beat upgraded guidance of 385MT; driven by higher brine volumes at central Oklahoma facilities + IO#11 expansion + higher brine temperatures.

**H2 2026 outlook:** 460–485MT production guided; IO#12 (largest IOsorb® plant; commissioning Q3 2026) is the key driver. Multi-year ambition: ~5% of world output (~1,750MT), roughly 2x current annual run-rate, including a Permian Basin expansion.

**Critical-material angle:** Iodine is on some US critical-material watchlists (X-ray contrast media, LCD polarizers, biocides, pharma — $70+/kg spot = significant US domestic supply premium implied). But this narrative has no hard "supply-security scaffolding" (no DoD offtake, no price floor, no strategic contract) that would convert it into a durable multiple expansion.

## Skeptic's Confirmation Checklist (10 Items)

1. **Revenue recognition** — **CONFIRMED (low risk).** Physical crystalline iodine sold to industrial buyers/distributors, booked on delivery at prevailing spot/negotiated prices. No milestone conditions, no %-completion tricks. Clean.

2. **Revenue durability (can it be lost?)** — **PLAUSIBLE.** Volume is durable (structural demand; Iofina is growing market share). Value can compress sharply — revenue could halve on a 50% price decline without any operational failure. Revenue "won't disappear" but can move violently with iodine.

3. **Competitive capture (sole-source true?)** — **RED-FLAG.** The "near-sole-source US producer" premise is overstated. Woodward Iodine (Ise Chemicals subsidiary), IOCHEM, and Kiva Corp also produce US oilfield-brine iodine commercially. Iofina has a process cost/efficiency edge and is the most active US grower, but it is not the exclusive domestic source. IOsorb® is a competitive advantage, not a monopoly.

4. **Moat stickiness over time** — **PLAUSIBLE.** Patented membrane-diffusion process is a genuine efficiency edge. In-source risk mitigated (Iofina works with operators via surface-rights agreements, not in competition with them). Long-run patent cliff and potential for process replication by well-capitalized rivals (SQM, ACF/Algorta) if iodine stays >$60/kg long enough to justify CapEx. Brine temperature dependence (H1 2026 upside partly from "higher brine temperatures") is an uncontrollable input.

5. **Customer concentration** — **UNVERIFIED.** Iodine's end-market is concentrated in a few global distributor/converter relationships. Historical Iofina has used agency/marketing arrangements. Specific customer concentration not granularly disclosed in accessible documents. This is a genuine gap — if 1–2 distributors handle >50% of sales, sudden non-renewal is a meaningful risk.

6. **Pricing power** — **RED-FLAG.** None independent. Iofina is a global commodity price-taker. Today's 18% EBITDA margin is **shortage rent from $70+/kg iodine pricing** — not durable pricing power. Historical margins at $20–30/kg iodine were thin/negative. Management appropriately does not claim pricing power.

7. **Accounting quality** — **PLAUSIBLE (watch).** Statutory numbers appear clean. Note: adj-EBITDA add-backs exist; H1 2025 PBT *excluding* government subsidies was only $1.66M (vs. reported £3.5M) — some prior profit was subsidy-aided, not pure operations. Track statutory vs. adjusted carefully in future results.

8. **Ownership & dilution** — **RED-FLAG.** Very low insider ownership (CEO 0.073%); history of share issuance to fund prior IOsorb plant builds; expansion pipeline (IO#12, Permian) is capital-hungry. At current $11.7M net cash with a multi-hundred-million-dollar volume ambition, equity issuance is probable over the next 2–3 years unless iodine spot stays elevated.

9. **Input/supply dependency** — **PLAUSIBLE.** Brine supply is from third-party oilfield operators under surface/access agreements. Output depends on operators' drilling activity and brine volumes. H1 2026 upside was explicitly from "higher brine volumes AND higher brine temperatures" — both outside Iofina's direct control. Risk: if Oklahoma oil production declines, brine supply and brine quality decline with it. Mitigated by multiple field locations (Oklahoma + Colorado + Montana) and Permian expansion.

10. **Management credibility** — **CONFIRMED.** Beat *upgraded* H1 2026 guidance (393.3MT vs. 385MT); delivered record FY2025; has executed multiple plant builds (IO#7 through IO#12) broadly on plan. Record of delivering vs. guidance is strong and consistent over the past 3 years.

**Tally: 2 CONFIRMED · 4 PLAUSIBLE · 1 UNVERIFIED · 3 RED-FLAG**

The three red flags attack the quality/moat framing (sole-source overstatement, pricing power, low alignment + dilution risk), not the operational execution. The UNVERIFIED (customer concentration) is a genuine risk that could change the thesis.

## Historical Base Rates (3 Analogs)

The correct comp set is **coverage-thin, low-cost byproduct/waste-stream chemical processors that rode a commodity spike** — not gold miners.

**1. Sylvania Platinum (SLP.L)** — retreats PGM from chrome tailings (closest structural twin: patented process, someone else's waste stream, AIM-listed, genuine low-cost edge). Re-rated hard on the 2020–21 PGM price spike (+3–4x). Then **de-rated ~60–70% when PGM prices reverted in 2022–23**, despite steady operations and no operational failures. The multiple never re-rated to "quality franchise" — the market always priced earnings as cyclical. Down-move was iodine-price-driven, not execution-driven.

**2. Kenmare Resources (KMR.L)** — low-cost ilmenite/titanium dioxide byproduct-style producer; AIM-listed. Earnings and multiple both swung *up* with pigment prices and *down* together in synchronized fashion. Coverage was thin at the bottom (re-rating opportunity), rich at the top (when the KB would be buying) — the classic "always late" trap for commodity re-rates.

**3. MP Materials / Lynas (bull counter-analog)** — US/ex-China rare-earth processors that *did* achieve a **durable structural multiple re-rate** — but only because explicit **government/critical-minerals supply-security scaffolding** got permanently attached: US DoD offtake agreements, IRA/CHIPS Act subsidies, price floors in practice. Iofina has the *narrative* (US-domestic, critical material listed) but **none of the hard scaffolding** (no DoD deal, no price floor, no strategic long-term offtake). The critical-minerals premium is hypothetical for IOF.L; it is contractual for Lynas/MP.

**Base rate:** Byproduct/waste-stream processors (Sylvania, Kenmare) re-rate on the commodity spike, maintain a low cyclical multiple (3–5x EV/EBITDA), then de-rate hard on price reversal — Iofina has already captured the up-leg; a *durable* multiple re-rate requires Lynas/MP-style supply-security scaffolding that Iofina does not yet have. Historical analogs re-rated 2 of 3 initially, then de-rated on commodity reversal 2 of 2; swing factor = commodity price, not process quality.

## Macro Context (added to Macro Observations)

IOF.L illustrates a recurring pattern: the best coverage-void entry in a byproduct-process niche occurs at cyclical trough pricing when the stock is universally ignored. Once the commodity re-rates AND the operations beat, the coverage void closes and the stock re-rates. By the time a systematic screener finds the name (it appeared in our sector-0 3rd pass at £50.84M cap on 22% revenue growth), the easy money is largely done. **The lesson: for commodity-exposed niche processors, run the coverage-void screen more aggressively at commodity troughs, not after the spike.**
